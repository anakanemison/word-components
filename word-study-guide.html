Study Guide

English Word Roots, Prefixes, and Suffixes

English vocabulary is a blend of older Germanic words and later borrowings from Latin and Greek. Learning common word parts makes it easier to guess meanings, decode unfamiliar terms, and remember new vocabulary more quickly.

Roots carry a word's core meaning.
Prefixes come at the beginning and modify meaning.
Suffixes come at the end and often change function or tone.

12 Common Roots

Roots are meaning-bearing building blocks. Some stand alone, while others appear mostly inside larger words.

Latin Roots

Often found in academic, legal, and formal English vocabulary.

port carryExamples: transport, portable
scrib/script writeExamples: describe, manuscript
rupt breakExamples: interrupt, rupture
vid/vis seeExamples: video, visible

Greek Roots

Very common in science, medicine, and technical vocabulary.

bio lifeExamples: biology, biography
chron timeExamples: chronology, chronometer
phon soundExamples: telephone, phonics
therm heatExamples: thermal, thermometer

Old English / Germanic Roots

These often survive as everyday words and as parts of compounds.

craft skill or artExamples: handicraft, craftsmanship
folk peopleExamples: folklore, folksong
ward guard or protectExamples: warden, steward
work labor or doExamples: workshop, homework

12 Common Prefixes

Prefixes come before a base word and shift its direction, degree, position, or attitude.

Latin Prefixes

These often create precise, formal, or academic shades of meaning.

pre- beforeExamples: preview, prehistoric
sub- under or belowExamples: submarine, subzero
inter- between or amongExamples: interact, international
trans- across or beyondExamples: transport, transatlantic

Greek Prefixes

Common in technical, scientific, and descriptive terms.

anti- against or oppositeExamples: antibiotic, antisocial
hyper- over, above, or excessiveExamples: hyperactive, hypertension
mono- one or singleExamples: monologue, monochrome
tele- far or distantExamples: telephone, telework

Old English / Germanic Prefixes

These are especially common in everyday English verbs and compounds.

fore- before or in frontExamples: forecast, forehead
over- above or excessivelyExamples: overcook, overreact
under- below or insufficientlyExamples: underground, underpay
mid- middleExamples: midday, midterm

12 Common Suffixes

Suffixes come after a base word and often mark a word as a noun, adjective, or idea category.

Latin / French Suffixes

These are common in formal writing and many school vocabulary words.

-able capable of or fit forExamples: readable, portable
-tion act, process, or resultExamples: creation, eruption
-ity state or qualityExamples: clarity, flexibility
-ous full of or havingExamples: curious, hazardous

Greek Suffixes

Common in disciplines, belief systems, and specialized vocabulary.

-ism belief, system, or practiceExamples: realism, activism
-ist person who practices or studiesExamples: artist, biologist
-logy study ofExamples: geology, zoology
-phobia fear of or strong aversion toExamples: arachnophobia, xenophobia

Old English / Germanic Suffixes

These often feel familiar because they remain active in everyday word-building.

-hood state or conditionExamples: childhood, neighborhood
-ship status, skill, or relationshipExamples: friendship, leadership
-dom state, condition, or realmExamples: freedom, kingdom
-ful full ofExamples: joyful, careful
Note: English word histories can be layered, and many forms entered English through French or Latin after earlier use in Greek or Germanic languages.
